We all know the Great Wall of China in the ancient world, whose length is more than 8,000 km, but the Firewall of China, which is called in the global jargon of technology people `Great Firewall of China (GFW)`, we have not heard so much about, or at least we have heard but did not know in depth. This is the beauty of this wall, it exists and is very strong but hidden from most of the world. This new wall is not different in scale, volume and investment that were made in the ancient world and the physical wall, and it has been under construction, maintenance and strengthening for already 30 years.
<br><br>
 {% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/The Great Firewall of China/01.jpg" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" %}
<br>
This Great Firewall is in fact one of the most sophisticated network censorship systems in the world, based on infrastructure distributed at key points in the international fiber connections to China and operates in a centralized manner. It combines a variety of techniques to block unwanted traffic. It was developed already at the beginning of the 90s of the twentieth century, and its purpose is to control the flow of information between the global internet network and China. This last sentence is extremely important because it is the foundation and upon it rests all the philosophy and work around this wall – control over information, where the resulting outcome of this is control over the opinion of the residents of China.
<br><br>

## Ideology and Cyber Controls: The Justification for the Firewall
The Chinese government, including President Xi Jinping himself, has praised China’s filtered internet model as a mechanism that protects the country’s citizens, and it has even been suggested more than once that it should serve as a global model for managing cyberspace. The government justifies the use of this censorship system as a national necessity for maintaining public security and state stability, defining it as “cyber controls” and the protection of borders in the online space. Government officials in various discussions claim that blocking content protects citizens from “harmful” or dangerous information, and ensures the national well-being of society. According to official statements, the goal is to filter content that promotes terrorism, violence, pornography, the spread of harmful rumors, and political subversion, thereby protecting public order and China’s values.
<br><br>
Accordingly, the state conducts periodic campaigns to clean the internet of inappropriate content and emphasizes that these measures are taken for the safety of the public. In other words, the firewall is presented to the Chinese population as a tool of cultural self-defense and as a kind of “digital shield” that protects China’s values from harmful Western influences that attempt to change their culture from within.
<br><br>
Since the firewall operates filtering at the national level, Chinese citizens and foreigners, including tourists, who browse from within China experience similar restrictions in terms of blocked websites. In practice, any internet connection that passes through Chinese communication infrastructure is subject to limitations: popular global websites such as Google, Facebook, YouTube, and the social network X are completely blocked for users within China. However, there are certain differences in the way content is presented to different audiences. Government media outlets distribute different versions of the narrative – a more prominent propagandistic tone internally, compared to more refined wording in English for an international audience. In addition, Chinese platforms, which are usually controlled in one way or another by the regime, differentiate between Chinese user accounts and foreign ones. For example, the messaging application WeChat censors more strictly accounts registered with a Chinese number compared to accounts registered with a foreign number.
<br><br>

## Shaping Consciousness: The Impact on the Younger Generation
This policy has dramatically changed the way information is consumed and how knowledge is formed among the Chinese public. Disconnection from the global flow has created a generation that consumes information mainly from internal Chinese sources and under full control of the state. Studies have found that strict censorship creates a situation in which many citizens are not at all aware of what they do not see. In practice, making access to external information more difficult reduces the demand for it, and only a very small percentage of users in China actively try to bypass the blocking. According to estimates, only about 1% of all Chinese internet users use bypass tools such as unauthorized VPNs or the Tor network in order to consume blocked content. The vast majority are satisfied with the local information available to them and use convenient Chinese platforms such as WeChat, which are controlled by the government. From this we learn that China has succeeded in its project, which is imposing order and controlling the flow of information consumed within the country.
<br><br>
In the socio-political dimension, analysts point to a noticeable change in the approach of the younger generation in China toward the world and toward the government under the strict censorship regime combined with propaganda. National feelings among young people have strengthened, and in the last decade of Xi Jinping’s rule there has been an increase in the tightening of censorship alongside the delivery of intensive patriotic messages. According to reports by human rights organizations, educated young people, who in the past tended more toward critical thinking and openness to ideas from outside, have become more aligned with the national narrative of the Chinese government.
<br><br>

## Continuous Citizen Monitoring and the Human Censorship Mechanism
Beyond the technical blocking of websites, a central part of the Chinese system is the constant monitoring of user activity on the internet. In China, censorship is not “blind” to the identity of users, and the government aims to know who said what online. For this purpose, laws and regulations were enacted that require telecommunications and internet companies in China to assist in monitoring. A basic rule is the requirement for identification and verification, which means that anonymous use of the internet is almost not possible. Internet providers and content platforms were required by law to enforce real-name registration of users, so that every user in China is required to identify himself with an ID number when purchasing a SIM card for a phone, opening an account on a social network, or writing a comment in a forum.
<br><br>
 {% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/The Great Firewall of China/02.jpg" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" %}
<br>
In addition, data retention is a requirement mandated by law, and internet providers must document browsing logs and user activity for periods much longer than is common in the Western world, and make them available to authorities upon request. The result of this is that every website or application in China maintains internal control mechanisms, and they filter content and monitor chats, and when problematic content is identified, they not only delete it but sometimes also report it to censorship authorities.
<br><br>
Data retention is not the final statement, and the Chinese government operates powerful automation through a human army of censors. According to official reports, already in 2013 about 2 million workers were employed in internet censorship and monitoring mechanisms, and their number has only grown since. There is a phenomenon that has been documented on social networks more than once and is known as the “50 Cent Army” (Wu Mao Dang), which are in fact paid commentators on behalf of the government, whose role is to flood the internet with pro-government messages and to steer discussions toward the narrative that the government wants.
<br><br>
Through a combination of real-name identification, automated tools, and an army of censors, China is able to monitor personal internet activity at a scale that has no precedent in the world and in unimaginable numbers. If a Chinese user uploads a critical post or shares prohibited information, it is very likely that the post will be deleted quickly and may also lead to a visit by law enforcement authorities and imprisonment. Most users in China are very aware that authorities watch and monitor them, and therefore simply avoid in advance publishing or searching for sensitive political materials.
<br><br>
Operating an online censorship system for a population of more than 1.4 billion people is an enormous task that requires massive investment of technological and human resources, and China indeed allocates unprecedented resources for this purpose. According to a study by Reporters Without Borders (RSF), national spending on internet censorship in China was estimated in 2020 at more than 6.6 billion dollars, and these funds are mainly intended to finance the purchase of advanced equipment, development of algorithms, and employment of manpower and more. Truly the operation of a large and well-prepared system.
<br><br>
This investment has been yielding results for more than 15 years. There are reports that on a daily basis, the system handles enormous amounts of censorship events. For example, at the peak of the COVID period in the world, between January and September 2020, the Chinese Cyberspace Administration (CAC) reported the closure of about 130,000 social media accounts and about 12,000 websites due to content violations, and it is very possible that the actual number of blocks was much higher than reported.
<br><br>
The GFW system also creates engineering challenges: the very operation of filtering all network traffic in the country places a significant load on bandwidth and creates bottlenecks. More than once, slowdowns in international browsing from China were measured as a result of the load of the firewall, a phenomenon that system planners try to reduce using stronger hardware, use of advanced algorithms, and wide deployment among internet providers.
<br><br>

## The Technological Architecture: How Does the Filtering Mechanism Work?
The question arises, so how exactly does the powerful and efficient content filtering mechanism of the Chinese government work? For this purpose, we will review some of the methods that have been proven to be in use, where each one covers the weaknesses of another method:
<br><br>

### 1) Blocking based on keywords or domains:
A classic method operates at the DNS level and also at the HTTP content or TLS handshake level. A DPI mechanism scans the first request for forbidden words or expressions such as opposition groups, censored events, or political insults. If found in the URL, request body, or headers like Host, blocking is triggered immediately. A 2021 study showed the firewall uses around 15,000 domain-related keywords for DNS filtering.
<br>
In encrypted traffic, the URL content cannot be read, but filtering by domain is still possible using the SNI field, where the client specifies the target hostname in the TLS request. China has long censored HTTPS similarly to HTTP by inspecting the TLS Client Hello and blocking blacklisted domains. This allows blocking sites like Google, Facebook, and YouTube even over HTTPS, since their domain appears in the SNI.
<br><br>

### 2) Algorithms for Identifying Encrypted or Suspicious Traffic:
When the firewall faces a challenge where the traffic is encrypted end-to-end or obfuscated, so that no visible text remains for keyword inspection. In such cases, the GFW operates Traffic Fingerprinting algorithms, which mean an attempt to understand and identify characteristics of traffic flow in order to try to infer the nature of suspicious traffic without decrypting the encryption. 
<br>
One of the newer mechanisms discovered is a system for identifying fully encrypted protocols such as Shadowsocks, V2Ray, Tor and others, and instead of “breaking” the encryption, the system applies a set of heuristic rules that examine the structure of the first packets in the connection and compare them to statistical characteristics of “normal” traffic such as a known pattern of HTTP, standard TLS, SSH, and more. In addition to passive analysis, more active mechanisms are also applied, such as protocol pattern comparison and statistical analysis of traffic flow
<br><br>

### 3) Blocking of Protocols and Ports:
Beyond content filtering, the GFW operates mechanisms for blocking specific protocols (such as Tor, VPN, SSH, HTTP/S) and even blocking certain communication ports, with the purpose of disrupting the use of tools for bypassing censorship.
<br>
The identification of a protocol is based on several methods, where first of all, there are blocklists (blacklists) of IP addresses and key servers. For example, already in 2009 all public Tor relays were removed from the Chinese network after the authorities extracted the list of addresses from the central Tor server and blocked them all. Also, websites of popular VPN services are blocked at the domain name level as well as by IP addresses (including DNS blocking and null routing to their addresses) in order to prevent even access to downloading the tools. In addition, the firewall maintains in its lists additional key indicators – for example, it is known that common ports used by circumvention protocols (such as a known VPN port) are under strict monitoring, and in certain cases may also be proactively blocked. When an attempt to communicate using a forbidden protocol is identified, the system takes blocking actions almost immediately.
<br><br>

### 4) Control and Intelligence Systems, Machine Learning:
There is an ongoing question whether the system relies on machine learning or human-defined rules. Research over the years shows it is mainly based on manual filtering rules and response mechanisms set by humans. Evidence suggests that GFW filters (DNS, keywords, DPI) rely on predefined and periodically updated blocklists of words and websites, indicating a deterministic, deliberately designed system rather than independent learning.
<br>
This does not exclude the use of AI. Long-term traffic analysis can help detect new censorship bypass methods, which are then manually added to blocklists. The GFW can therefore be seen as a hybrid system: core manual rules, regularly updated with insights from automated data analysis.
<br>
Another layer of “intelligence” is centralized control, allowing fast responses to events. For example, during sensitive political periods, censorship may suddenly intensify, blocking services that were previously accessible. This suggests active human oversight supported by alert systems that monitor traffic, blocked attempts, and risk indicators. Operators can tighten or relax filtering as needed, and new circumvention tools are often quickly identified and blocked once they gain popularity.
<br><br>

### 5) Great Cannon, Code Injection into Existing Traffic:
In addition to filtering, China operates an offensive tool called the “Great Cannon,” first exposed after a March 2015 cyberattack targeting anti-censorship sites like GitHub and GreatFire.org. Researchers found a separate system alongside the firewall that can intercept normal traffic and inject malicious code. Unlike the passive firewall, which blocks or slows traffic, the Great Cannon can hijack user connections and turn them into attack tools.
<br>
In the 2015 case, users accessing Baidu ad servers received injected malicious JavaScript instead of the original content, causing their computers to flood GitHub with requests, effectively turning them into DDoS “zombies.” Technically separate from the GFW but sharing similar infrastructure, the Great Cannon can replace unencrypted traffic and inject any content, including malware. This represents a major escalation, enabling active offensive operations, not just defensive filtering. Beyond DDoS, such capabilities could also be used for espionage, misinformation, or other cyberattacks.
<br><br>

<br>

### 6) TCP-RST packet injection mechanism for connection termination:
 This is a very effective enforcement tool of the Chinese government, which in practice performs injection of forged TCP Reset (RST) packets to terminate unwanted connections. After a DPI mechanism or any other detection decides that a given connection should be blocked, the GFW does not “instruct” the router to stop the packets (since the system operates alongside the traffic and not as a direct inline barrier), but rather actively sends a series of TCP RST packets to both directions of the connection.
<br>
RST packets are one-sided termination messages in the TCP protocol, and when one side receives them, its TCP stack interprets this as if the other side closed the connection on its own initiative. In practice, whenever blocked content or protocol is detected, the Chinese router immediately generates 3–4 forged RST/ACK packets sent back both to the Chinese client and to the remote server in parallel. When both sides (client and server) disconnect, the blocking is effectively achieved without the firewall having to physically “block” the line, which simplifies enforcement and system management.
<br><br>

### 7) DNS poisoning and spoofing:
DNS is a key target in the firewall’s first filtering layer. The GFW blocks access to forbidden sites before a connection is established by preventing domain names from resolving to correct IP addresses, mainly through forged DNS responses. When a user queries a domain, the GFW intercepts the request, typically at international gateways, and if the domain is censored, it sends a fake response faster than the real server. Since DNS uses UDP without session validation, a forged reply with an incorrect IP or NXDOMAIN is accepted as legitimate.
<br>
About 77,000 blocked domains had such forged records leak to public DNS servers. Local DNS servers in China are also configured to fail queries for blocked domains, so users often cannot resolve them at all. Attempts to bypass DNS using direct IP access trigger other filtering layers.
<br>
This method often blocks entire subdomains, meaning platforms like GitHub Pages or WordPress.com may have all subdomains blocked, causing collateral damage. With the rise of DNS-over-HTTPS and DNS-over-TLS, China also blocks major DoH providers like Google and Cloudflare by targeting their IPs.
<br><br>

## Global and geopolitical implications
After understanding the philosophy behind the online wall that was built, its effects, and the technological methods through which the government achieves the desired blocking, we will try to step a bit outward and understand how this policy affects other countries in the free world. Democratic countries and digital rights organizations have for years criticized China for suppressing freedom of expression on the internet, and sometimes also try to help Chinese citizens bypass the restrictions.
<br><br>
United States has invested over the past decades millions of dollars in supporting the development of circumvention tools (VPNs, the Tor network, projects like Psiphon, and similar), with the goal of enabling free flow of information into China. Other Western countries, including the United States, are increasingly taking restrictive regulatory measures against Chinese technologies. Democratic governments claim that Chinese applications have “entered” their internet space while exploiting the freedom of information practiced in them, and carried out influence campaigns on public opinion and on the content exposed to citizens, something that of course cannot happen under the Chinese firewall, and from here this is a battle of minds and influence that only China can conduct toward the world without consequences for its own citizens.
<br><br>
While the United States takes a firm approach, the European Union has adopted a different regulatory approach. Europe approved broad laws such as the Digital Services Act (DSA) and the Digital Markets Act (DMA), which impose transparency obligations on platforms regarding algorithms and content. These laws will also apply to Chinese companies if they operate in the European market. In addition, countries such as India, Japan, and Australia have increased oversight of Chinese technologies within their territories, some even banning entire applications. These measures are not identical to a firewall and are based on security and privacy considerations rather than a desire to censor internal political information, but the result is that the online space is becoming more fragmented along geopolitical lines.
<br><br>

## Freedom vs Suppression
The Great Firewall system of China is much more than a technical tool for blocking sites. From the Chinese perspective, it is seen as necessary to maintain regime stability and cultural identity, even in the digital age. The cost is viewed in the Western world as harm to the freedom of information of more than a billion people and the isolation of China from the global knowledge and discussion sphere. In the technological dimension, the firewall stands at the forefront of online censorship engineering, using a combination of multiple mechanisms at an unprecedented operational scale. Its global effects are very noticeable, from strengthening local tech companies in China at the expense of international ones, through increasing technological and security tensions with the United States, to inspiring other regimes to build similar “walls.” 
<br><br>
Over the years and after many studies and analyses, it appears that the Chinese public has largely adapted to a restricted internet reality, and many in the population, especially those born from the 1990s onward, do not seek alternatives at all, and some even internalize the limited narratives imposed by the censor.
In this sense, the Chinese firewall is a very strong and impressive system, but not completely immune to vulnerabilities and new technological changes. The struggle between freedom and suppression on the network, symbolized by the Chinese cyber wall, continues to shape the future of the internet and sharpens the value differences between China and the Western world.
